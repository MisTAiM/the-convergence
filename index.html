Pattern 03 — Currency Debasement → Collapse
ROME: THE TEMPLATE

The Roman denarius went from 90% silver (100 CE) to 5% silver (270 CE) over 170 years. Each debasement funded short-term military spending. Each debasement made the next crisis worse. The final collapse (476 CE) was not caused by barbarian invasion — the barbarians had been inside the empire for generations. It was caused by an economy so hollowed out it could no longer pay its own defense.

US DOLLAR: THE CURRENT PATTERN

The dollar has been debased through money printing, not silver shaving. M2 money supply: $4.6T (2000) → $21.7T (2024). US Debt/GDP: 33% (2000) → 118% (2024). Each crisis since 2008 has required more money creation to stabilize than the last. The structural mathematics are identical to Rome — the timeline is compressed by the speed of modern finance.

THE KEY DIFFERENCE

Rome had no alternative. The dollar has competitors: euro, yuan, BRICS Pay, gold-backed instruments, and eventually CBDCs. The dollar does not need to "collapse" — it needs only to lose reserve primacy. That transition, once begun, is self-reinforcing: as dollar holdings decline, the cost of dollar debt increases, which increases the debasement pressure, which accelerates the reserve shift. The UK pound completed this sequence in 43 years (1913–1956).

How Pattern Confidence Is Calculated

Each detected pattern is assigned a confidence score based on: (1) strength of the current signal vs. historical baseline, (2) number of independent confirming data streams, (3) historical hit rate of similar patterns in comparable circumstances. The history log is stored exclusively in localStorage — a browser-side storage mechanism that never leaves your device. No data is sent to any server. No account is required. A Note on Prediction Epistemology

The goal of prediction is not certainty — it is calibration. A well-calibrated prediction system is one where 70% confidence events happen roughly 70% of the time. Overconfident predictions (claiming certainty where there is probability) are more dangerous than honest uncertainty. Every prediction here includes the specific data sources and historical precedents that generate the confidence score — so you can evaluate the reasoning, not just accept the conclusion. Disagreement with a well-sourced prediction is a productive analytical exercise. Acceptance without understanding is not.
